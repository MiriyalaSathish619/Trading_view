Learn about electronic share storage.

A Demat account, short for Dematerialized account, holds your shares and securities in electronic format, eliminating the need for physical certificates. It simplifies trading and investing by providing a secure, digital way to manage your portfolio. With a Demat account, you can easily buy, sell, and transfer shares, track your investments in real-time, and reduce risks like loss or damage of physical documents. It’s typically linked to a trading account and a bank account for seamless transactions.

Understand the difference.

No, a Demat account and a trading account serve different purposes. A Demat account stores your securities, such as shares and bonds, in electronic form, acting like a digital vault. A trading account, on the other hand, is used to place buy or sell orders in the stock market. While the Demat account holds your assets, the trading account facilitates transactions. You need both for online trading: the trading account to execute trades and the Demat account to hold or receive the securities.

Discover key advantages.

An ATS Demat Account offers multiple benefits for investors. It provides seamless integration with trading platforms, allowing real-time access to market data and trading tools. The account ensures secure storage of assets with advanced encryption, protecting your investments. ATS offers zero hidden charges, transparent pricing, and low brokerage fees. Additional perks include instant portfolio tracking, automated dividend credits, and dedicated customer support to assist with account management and trading queries.

Compare trading types.

Intraday brokerage applies to trades where you buy and sell securities within the same trading day, aiming to profit from short-term price movements. The brokerage fee is typically lower due to the high volume of trades. Options brokerage, however, applies to trading derivatives like options contracts, where you pay a premium for the right to buy or sell an asset at a set price. Options brokerage can vary based on contract size and market volatility, often higher than intraday due to complexity.

Trading Indicators

Enhance your market analysis with our powerful and customizable trading indicators.

Moving Average (MA)

Smooths price data to identify trends over a specified period. Choose between Simple (SMA) and Exponential (EMA).

Use Case: Helps identify trend direction and potential reversals.

Formula: SMA = Sum of closing prices / Period

Tip: MA crossovers (e.g., 50 SMA crossing 200 SMA) are commonly used signals.

Relative Strength Index (RSI)

Measures momentum to identify overbought or oversold conditions. Typically ranges between 0 and 100.

Use Case: Identifies overbought (>70) or oversold (<30) conditions to predict reversals.

Formula: RSI = 100 - (100 / (1 + RS)), where RS = Average Gain / Average Loss over Period

Tip: Look for RSI divergence with price to spot potential trend reversals.

MACD

Shows the relationship between two EMAs to identify momentum and trend changes.

Use Case: Detects momentum shifts and trend changes through MACD and signal line crossovers.

Formula: MACD = 12-Period EMA - 26-Period EMA; Signal = 9-Period EMA of MACD

Tip: A bullish signal occurs when MACD crosses above the signal line; bearish when below.

Bollinger Bands

Volatility indicator that uses a moving average and standard deviations to form bands.

Use Case: Identifies overbought or oversold conditions and market volatility.

Formula: Middle Band = SMA(Period); Upper Band = SMA + (StdDev * 2); Lower Band = SMA - (StdDev * 2)

Tip: Price touching the upper/lower band may indicate a reversal, especially with low volatility.

Stochastic Oscillator

Momentum indicator comparing a security’s closing price to its price range over a specific period.

Use Case: Detects potential reversal points based on momentum shifts.

Formula: %K = ((Current Close - Lowest Low) / (Highest High - Lowest Low)) * 100; %D = 3-Period SMA of %K

Tip: Look for %K/%D crossovers in overbought (>80) or oversold (<20) zones for trade signals.

Average True Range (ATR)

Measures market volatility by analyzing the range of price movements.

Use Case: Helps set stop-loss levels and gauge market volatility.

Formula: ATR = SMA of True Range over Period; True Range = Max(High - Low, |High - Prev Close|, |Low - Prev Close|)

Tip: Use ATR to adjust position sizing; higher ATR suggests wider stops.

Ichimoku Cloud

Advanced indicator combining trend, momentum, and support/resistance in one view.

Use Case: Provides a full picture of trend direction, strength, and support/resistance.

Formula: Tenkan-sen = (9-Period High + Low) / 2; Kijun-sen = (26-Period High + Low) / 2; Span A = (Tenkan + Kijun) / 2; Span B = (52-Period High + Low) / 2

Tip: Price above the cloud indicates a bullish trend; below suggests bearish.

Deep Dive Into Indicators

Moving Averages (MA)

What is it?

Moving Averages (MAs) are statistical tools that calculate the average price of an asset over a specific period, smoothing out short-term price fluctuations to reveal trends. They are widely used in technical analysis to identify market direction and potential trading opportunities.

How it Works

MAs are calculated by averaging the closing prices of an asset over a chosen number of periods (e.g., 20 days). The result is plotted on a chart as a line that follows the price, helping traders identify trends and potential reversal points.

  • Simple Moving Average (SMA): SMA = (P1 + P2 + ... + Pn) / n, where P is the price and n is the number of periods. Equal weight is given to all prices.
  • Exponential Moving Average (EMA): EMA = (Current Price × (2 / (n + 1))) + (Previous EMA × (1 - (2 / (n + 1)))). Recent prices have more weight, making it more responsive.
  • Weighted Moving Average (WMA): Assigns higher weights to recent prices, calculated as WMA = (P1 × w1 + P2 × w2 + ... + Pn × wn) / (w1 + w2 + ... + wn).

Interpretation: A rising MA indicates an uptrend, while a falling MA suggests a downtrend. Crossovers between MAs can signal buy or sell opportunities.

Types

  • SMA: Best for long-term trends due to its simplicity and stability.
  • EMA: Ideal for short-term trading as it reacts faster to price changes.
  • WMA: Less common but useful for traders prioritizing recent price action.

Use Cases

  • Trend Identification: Price above a 200-period SMA indicates a bullish trend; below suggests a bearish trend.
  • Crossover Strategy: A “Golden Cross” (50 EMA crossing above 200 EMA) is a buy signal; a “Death Cross” (50 EMA crossing below 200 EMA) is a sell signal.
  • Support and Resistance: The 50 or 200 SMA often acts as dynamic support in uptrends or resistance in downtrends.
  • Confluence Zones: Combine MAs with RSI or MACD for stronger signals (e.g., buy when price is above 50 EMA and RSI > 70).

Setting Up in TradingView

  1. Open TradingView (tradingview.com).
  2. Click the “Indicators” button (fx icon) at the top.
  3. Search for “Moving Average” and select “Moving Average” (for SMA) or “Moving Average Exponential” (for EMA).
  4. Adjust the period (e.g., 50 for 50 SMA) and customize color/style in the settings.
  5. In the strategy builder, select “MA” as the indicator, choose an operator (e.g., >), and enter a value (e.g., price or another MA).

Example Strategy: EMA Crossover for Day Trading

Setup: Use a 9 EMA and 21 EMA on a 15-minute chart for NASDAQ:AAPL.

  1. Buy Signal: When the 9 EMA crosses above the 21 EMA, and price is above both EMAs.
  2. Sell Signal: When the 9 EMA crosses below the 21 EMA, or price falls below both EMAs.
  3. Confirmation: Use RSI > 50 for buy signals to avoid false crossovers.
  4. In the strategy builder: Add condition “MA > MA” (9 EMA > 21 EMA) and “RSI > 50”.

Result: This strategy targets short-term trends with quick entries and exits.

Limitations

  • Lagging Indicator: MAs react to price changes after they occur, leading to delayed signals.
  • Choppy Markets: MAs can produce false signals in sideways markets due to frequent crossovers.
  • Period Sensitivity: Choosing the wrong period (e.g., too short or too long) can reduce effectiveness.

Pro Tips

  • Use multiple MAs (e.g., 9, 21, 50) to confirm trends across timeframes.
  • Combine MAs with volume indicators to validate breakouts.
  • For day trading, test faster MAs (e.g., 9 or 13 EMA); for swing trading, use 50 or 100 SMA.
  • Adjust MA periods based on the asset’s volatility (e.g., shorter periods for crypto).

Frequently Asked Questions

Which MA period should I use?

It depends on your trading style: 9 or 21 for day trading, 50 for swing trading, and 200 for long-term investing. Test different periods on historical data.

Can MAs be used alone?

MAs are more effective when combined with other indicators (e.g., RSI, MACD) to confirm signals and reduce false positives.

Why do I get false signals in choppy markets?

MAs rely on trends, so in sideways markets, frequent price swings cause unreliable crossovers. Use oscillators like RSI in such conditions.

Relative Strength Index (RSI)

What is it?

RSI (Relative Strength Index) is a momentum oscillator that measures the speed and change of price movements. It ranges from 0 to 100 and helps traders identify overbought or oversold conditions in the market.

How it Works

RSI compares the magnitude of recent gains to recent losses over a specific period (typically 14). It's plotted as a line graph and helps indicate potential reversal points.

  • Formula: RSI = 100 - (100 / (1 + RS)), where RS = Average Gain / Average Loss.
  • Interpretation: RSI above 70 indicates overbought; below 30 indicates oversold. Traders look for reversals near these levels.

Types

  • Standard RSI: Most commonly set to 14 periods for balanced signals.
  • Modified RSI: Custom RSI settings (e.g., 5, 7, or 21) to adapt to specific asset volatility or timeframes.

Use Cases

  • Overbought/Oversold: RSI > 70 can indicate a potential sell zone, while RSI < 30 may indicate a buy zone.
  • Divergence: When price makes a new high but RSI does not, it may signal a reversal (bearish divergence); the opposite is bullish divergence.
  • Trend Confirmation: RSI staying above 50 during an uptrend or below 50 during a downtrend can validate trend strength.
  • Centerline Crossover: RSI crossing above 50 may confirm bullish momentum; below 50 suggests bearish momentum.

Setting Up in TradingView

  1. Go to tradingview.com.
  2. Click the “Indicators” button (fx icon).
  3. Search and select “Relative Strength Index (RSI)”.
  4. Adjust the length (default is 14) and customize levels (e.g., 30 and 70 lines).
  5. In the strategy builder, use RSI conditions like “RSI < 30” for buy or “RSI > 70” for sell.

Example Strategy: RSI Reversal

Setup: 14-period RSI on a 1-hour chart for NASDAQ:AAPL.

  1. Buy Signal: RSI crosses above 30 from oversold territory.
  2. Sell Signal: RSI crosses below 70 from overbought levels.
  3. Confirmation: Combine with price action patterns like double bottoms or candlestick reversal signals.
  4. In the strategy builder: Add “RSI < 30” followed by “RSI > 30” for entry; exit with “RSI > 70”.

Result: Helps catch reversals in range-bound or retracing markets.

Limitations

  • False Signals: In strong trends, RSI may remain overbought or oversold for extended periods.
  • Lagging Entry: Entry after a reversal signal can be delayed due to smoothing.
  • Not Trend-Specific: RSI works best in ranging markets, less so during strong trends unless combined with other tools.

Pro Tips

  • Use RSI with support/resistance levels or trendlines for better accuracy.
  • Look for divergence setups for high-probability reversal trades.
  • Adjust RSI period for faster or slower responses (e.g., 7 for aggressive trades, 21 for conservative).
  • Combine RSI with moving averages or Bollinger Bands for confluence-based strategies.

RSI is best in ranging or reversing markets. In trending markets, it's more reliable when combined with other indicators like moving averages.

MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence)

What is it?

The MACD is a trend-following momentum indicator that shows the relationship between two moving averages of an asset’s price. It helps traders identify potential buy/sell signals, trend strength, and momentum shifts.

How it Works

MACD is calculated by subtracting the 26-period EMA from the 12-period EMA. A 9-period EMA of the MACD (called the signal line) is then plotted on top. Traders watch for crossovers, divergence from price, and histogram shifts.

  • MACD Line: MACD = 12 EMA - 26 EMA
  • Signal Line: 9-period EMA of the MACD Line
  • Histogram: MACD - Signal Line, shows momentum

Interpretation: Crossovers between the MACD and Signal Line, or shifts in the histogram, can signal momentum changes or potential reversals.

Use Cases

  • MACD Crossover: Buy when MACD crosses above Signal; sell when it crosses below.
  • Histogram Reversal: A shrinking histogram may indicate weakening trend momentum.
  • Divergence: Price making new highs/lows while MACD fails to confirm can signal reversals.
  • Zero Line Cross: When MACD crosses the zero line, it may confirm a trend change.

Setting Up in TradingView

  1. Go to tradingview.com.
  2. Click the “Indicators” button (fx icon).
  3. Search for “MACD” and select it from the list.
  4. Adjust the fast, slow, and signal lengths (default: 12, 26, 9).
  5. In the strategy builder, use MACD crossover logic for entries/exits.

Example Strategy: MACD Histogram Momentum

Setup: Use MACD default (12, 26, 9) on 1-hour chart for NASDAQ:TSLA.

  1. Buy Signal: When MACD histogram flips from negative to positive, and MACD is above Signal Line.
  2. Sell Signal: When histogram flips back to negative, or MACD crosses below Signal.
  3. Confirmation: Use RSI > 50 or volume spike for confirmation.
  4. In the strategy builder: Add “MACD Histogram > 0” and “MACD > Signal”.

Result: A momentum-based strategy to catch early trend shifts.

Limitations

  • Lagging Indicator: MACD is based on moving averages, so signals come after the price moves.
  • Whipsaws: Can produce false signals in choppy or sideways markets.
  • Not Ideal for Scalping: Works better on higher timeframes due to smoother signals.

Pro Tips

  • Combine MACD with trendlines or support/resistance for stronger setups.
  • Use histogram analysis for early entry/exit signals.
  • Backtest MACD strategies across multiple assets and timeframes.
  • Adjust fast/slow lengths based on asset volatility (e.g., 5/35/5 for faster reaction).

Bollinger Bands

What is it?

Bollinger Bands are a technical analysis tool created by John Bollinger. They consist of a moving average (middle band) with two standard deviation lines (upper and lower bands) that expand or contract based on market volatility.

How it Works

Bollinger Bands help visualize volatility and potential reversal zones. The price tends to stay within the bands, and extreme moves near the bands may signal overbought/oversold conditions or breakout setups.

  • Middle Band: 20-period Simple Moving Average (SMA)
  • Upper Band: SMA + 2 standard deviations
  • Lower Band: SMA - 2 standard deviations

Interpretation: Bands widen during high volatility and contract during low volatility. Price touching bands can signal potential reversals or breakouts depending on context.

Use Cases

  • Bounce Trades: Price touches upper/lower band and reverts to mean (SMA).
  • Bollinger Squeeze: Bands tighten, signaling potential explosive breakout.
  • Band Walks: Strong trends can "walk" the bands — don’t fade without confirmation.
  • Divergence + Bands: Combine with RSI divergence for higher probability reversals at band extremes.

Setting Up in TradingView

  1. Go to tradingview.com.
  2. Click “Indicators” and search for “Bollinger Bands.”
  3. Select it to add to your chart (default: 20 period, 2 std dev).
  4. Customize period and deviation based on strategy (e.g., 10/2.5 for short-term moves).
  5. Use in confluence with support/resistance or candlestick patterns.

Example Strategy: Bollinger Bounce with Confirmation

Setup: 20/2 BB on 15-min chart for EUR/USD.

  1. Buy: Price touches lower band and forms a bullish engulfing candle.
  2. Sell: Price touches upper band and shows a bearish reversal pattern.
  3. Confirmation: RSI crosses 50 or MACD histogram flips.
  4. Exit: Target middle band or previous high/low.

Pro Tip: Avoid fading strong trends unless volatility is declining or divergence appears.

Limitations

  • False Signals: Touching bands doesn’t guarantee a reversal. Use confirmations.
  • Late Entries: In breakouts, price may move sharply after bands widen — react quickly or wait for retest.
  • Doesn’t Predict Direction: Bollinger Bands show volatility, not trend direction alone.

Pro Tips

  • Use in combination with volume or RSI for breakout/bounce confirmation.
  • Bollinger Band “Squeeze” is a powerful breakout signal — combine with momentum indicator.
  • Try adaptive settings: faster BBs for scalping, slower for swing trades.
  • Don’t use bands in isolation — confluence is key.

Stochastic Oscillator

What is it?

The Stochastic Oscillator is a momentum indicator that compares a security’s closing price to its price range over a set period. It helps identify overbought and oversold conditions and is often used for spotting trend reversals.

How it Works

Stochastic consists of two lines:

  • %K: The main line showing the current closing price relative to the recent high/low range.
  • %D: A smoothed version of %K (usually a 3-period SMA of %K).

It ranges from 0 to 100. Readings above 80 suggest overbought conditions, while below 20 suggests oversold. Crossovers between %K and %D are used as signals.

Use Cases

  • Overbought/Oversold Reversals: Look for crossovers when price is above 80 or below 20.
  • Divergence: Price makes new highs/lows, but the oscillator doesn’t — a potential reversal signal.
  • Trend Continuation: In strong trends, Stochastic may remain overbought or oversold — use with trend filters.
  • Pullback Entries: During trends, wait for Stochastic to reset before re-entering.

Setting Up in TradingView

  1. Visit tradingview.com.
  2. Click “Indicators” and search for “Stochastic.”
  3. Add it to your chart (default: 14, 3, 3 settings).
  4. Customize periods to suit strategy (e.g., 5, 3, 3 for faster signals).
  5. Use with trendlines, support/resistance, or other indicators.

Example Strategy: Stochastic + Trendline Reversal

Setup: 15-minute chart, Stochastic (14,3,3), trendlines drawn on price.

  1. Entry: Stochastic crosses upward from below 20 at trendline support.
  2. Exit: Near upper trendline or when Stochastic crosses down above 80.
  3. Stop-loss: Below swing low or beyond support.

Pro Tip: Combine with volume or candlestick patterns at crossover points.

Limitations

  • False Signals: In choppy markets, crossovers can mislead.
  • Lagging: It's reactive — use in context, not standalone.
  • Overuse: Being overbought/oversold doesn’t mean instant reversal — look for confirmation.

Pro Tips

  • Use with trendlines or channels for high-probability setups.
  • Try different settings: faster (5,3,3) for scalping, slower (21,9,9) for swings.
  • Combine with RSI or MACD for stronger confluence.
  • Stochastic divergence can signal early trend shifts — pair with chart patterns.

Average True Range (ATR)

What is it?

ATR (Average True Range) is a volatility indicator that measures how much an asset moves on average during a given time frame. It doesn’t indicate price direction, but rather how much price fluctuates, helping traders assess risk and set stop-loss levels.

How it Works

ATR is calculated using the True Range (TR), which is the greatest of:

  • Current High - Current Low
  • Absolute value of Current High - Previous Close
  • Absolute value of Current Low - Previous Close

The ATR is the moving average of the True Range over a set number of periods (usually 14).

Use Cases

  • Setting Stop-Loss: Use a multiple of ATR to place stop-loss beyond market noise.
  • Position Sizing: Higher ATR = higher volatility → reduce position size.
  • Volatility Breakouts: Sudden spikes in ATR may signal strong upcoming moves.
  • Trend Strength: Rising ATR during a move may confirm strength; falling ATR suggests consolidation.

Setting Up in TradingView

  1. Go to tradingview.com.
  2. Click on “Indicators” and search for “ATR.”
  3. Add it to your chart (default setting is 14 periods).
  4. Customize the period to match your trading timeframe if needed.
  5. Use ATR values directly or apply them in automated strategies.

Example Strategy: ATR-based Stop-Loss

Setup: Daily chart, ATR(14).

  1. Entry: Enter trade based on your usual signal (e.g., moving average crossover).
  2. Stop-loss: Place SL at 1.5×ATR below the entry (for long) or above (for short).
  3. Exit: Use trailing stop based on ATR or another signal.

Pro Tip: This dynamic SL adapts to volatility and avoids getting hit by random price noise.

Limitations

  • No Directional Signal: ATR doesn’t tell you if price will go up or down.
  • Lagging Indicator: Based on past price movement, not predictive.
  • Sudden Spikes: News or events can create temporary ATR surges — interpret with caution.

Pro Tips

  • Combine ATR with trend indicators for complete strategies.
  • Use different multipliers (1.5x, 2x ATR) depending on market conditions.
  • ATR trailing stops are popular in swing and trend-following systems.
  • Track ATR spikes after consolidation — they often precede big breakouts.

Ichimoku Cloud

What is it?

The Ichimoku Cloud (or Ichimoku Kinko Hyo) is a multifunctional indicator that shows support/resistance, trend direction, momentum, and potential trade signals — all in one glance. It's widely used in swing and trend-following strategies.

How it Works

Ichimoku consists of 5 lines:

  • Tenkan-sen (Conversion Line): (9-period high + low) / 2
  • Kijun-sen (Base Line): (26-period high + low) / 2
  • Senkou Span A: (Tenkan + Kijun) / 2, plotted 26 periods ahead
  • Senkou Span B: (52-period high + low) / 2, also plotted 26 periods ahead
  • Chikou Span: Current close plotted 26 periods back

The area between Span A and B forms the “Kumo” or Cloud — which helps visualize trend and support/resistance.

Use Cases

  • Trend Confirmation: Price above the cloud = uptrend; below = downtrend.
  • Dynamic Support/Resistance: The cloud acts as zones of support and resistance.
  • Signal Generation: Crossovers between Tenkan and Kijun lines, or price crossing the cloud, can act as signals.
  • Momentum Insights: A thick cloud = strong trend. Thin cloud = weak or consolidating market.

Setting Up in TradingView

  1. Go to tradingview.com.
  2. Click on “Indicators” and search for “Ichimoku Cloud.”
  3. Add it to your chart (default settings are 9, 26, 52).
  4. Customize the look by turning lines or cloud areas on/off based on your preference.

Example Strategy: Kumo Breakout

Setup: Use Ichimoku Cloud with default settings.

  1. Entry: Buy when price breaks above the cloud with Tenkan > Kijun and Chikou above price.
  2. Stop-loss: Below the lower span of the cloud (Senkou Span B).
  3. Exit: When price closes back below the cloud or Tenkan crosses below Kijun.

Pro Tip: Combine with volume spikes or momentum indicators to confirm breakouts.

Limitations

  • Complex Visuals: Can be overwhelming for beginners due to multiple lines.
  • Lagging: Cloud and signals are based on historical data, so they may lag in fast markets.
  • Not Ideal for Ranging Markets: Signals during sideways movement can be misleading.

Pro Tips

  • Use on higher timeframes (4H, Daily) for more reliable signals.
  • Combine with RSI or MACD to confirm momentum before entering.
  • Watch for “Kumo Twist” (when Span A and B cross) to signal trend changes ahead.
  • In strong trends, Kijun-sen can act as an ideal trailing stop-loss.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Ichimoku good for beginners?

It can be complex at first, but once understood, it offers a complete system. Start by focusing only on the cloud and Tenkan/Kijun lines.

What timeframe works best?

Daily and 4H timeframes provide the most reliable Ichimoku signals, especially for swing trading.

What does a flat Kijun or Senkou Span B mean?

Flat levels often act as magnets for price and strong support/resistance areas.
